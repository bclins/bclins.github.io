Week 1 Notes

We started with a 30 minute assessment pre-test that is not for a grade. After that we reviewed the rules for working with fractions.

  1. Addition. To add fractions, they need a common denominator (bottom). Once you have a common denominator, just add the numerators (tops). We did these examples:

    16+26 and 16+25\dfrac{1}{6} + \dfrac{2}{6} \text{ and }\dfrac{1}{6} + \dfrac{2}{5}

  2. Multiplication. I like to say that “fractions play nice with multiplication”. To multiply fractions, just multiply the tops and multiply the bottoms.

    16⋅25\dfrac{1}{6} \cdot \dfrac{2}{5}

  3. Division. Division is just multiplication by the reciprocal. You might have learned this as “keep-change-flip” or as “flip-and-multiply”. We did these examples:

    1625 and 165.\dfrac{ ~\dfrac{1}{6}~}{~\dfrac{2}{5}~ } \text { and } \dfrac{ ~\dfrac{1}{6}~}{~5~ }.

The graph of y=ax2+bx+cy = ax^2 + bx +c is a parabola.

  • The roots are where the graph crosses the xx-axis.

  • The vertex is where the parabola reaches its highest (or lowest) point. It is located at x=−b2a.x = \dfrac{-b}{2a}.

Week 9 Notes

This week we talked about using algebra to solve inequalities.

Function Crossing Rule

For two functions defined using the usual operations (++,−-,⋅\cdot,//), the function which is greater can only change at an xx-value where the graphs cross (are equal) or where one is undefined (a bad point).

Therefore you can solve complicated inequalities with these two steps:

  1. Mark the points of equality and any bad points on a number line.
  2. Check each resulting sub-interval to see if the inequality is true.

We used this method to solve these examples.

Week 11 Notes

This week was about working with exponents.

Understanding Exponents

  1. Powers represent repeated multiplication. an=a⋅a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⏟n copiesa^n = \underbrace{a \cdot a \cdot a \cdot \cdots \cdot a}_{n \text{ copies}}

  2. Negative powers represent reciprocals. a−n=1ana^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}

  3. Fraction powers represent roots. a1/n=ana^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}

From these basic ideas, you can work out all of the other exponent rules.

Powers Count Repeated Factors

Add powers of factors when you multiply. am⋅an=am+na^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}

Subtract powers when you divide. aman=am−n\dfrac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}

Powers Distribute to Factors

(a⋅b)n=an⋅bn and (ab)n=anbn(a \cdot b)^n = a^n \cdot b^n \text{ and } \left( \dfrac{a}{b} \right)^n = \dfrac{a^n}{b^n}

Powers do not distribute to terms. (a+b)n≠an+bn(a + b)^n \ne a^n + b^n

Power of a Power

(am)n=amn(a^m)^n = a^{mn}

Zero Power a0=1a^0 = 1

Week 12 Notes

Today we introduced logarithms. We started by talking about logarithmic scales, which are number lines where the numbers are spaced by multiplication instead of addition.

Logarithmic scales help understand the logarithm function.

What are Logarithms?

The logarithm function logb(x)\log_b(x) can be understood two ways:

  1. logb(x)\log_b(x) equals the number of steps xx is away from 1 on a (base-b) log-scale.

  2. logb(x)\log_b(x) equals the power of b needed to get xx.

  1. Find log216\log_2 16. (video)

  2. Find log381\log_3 81. (video)

  3. Find log1001\log_{100} 1. (video)

  4. Find log2(164)\log_2 \left( \frac{1}{64} \right). (video)

  5. Find log10(10).\log_{10} (\sqrt{10}).

Logarithms are useful because they convert difficult multiplication/division problems into easier addition/subtraction problems. They also convert exponential patterns into linear patterns.

Properties of Logarithms

  1. Logarithms convert multiplication to addition log(xy)=log(x)+log(y)\log(xy) = \log(x) + \log(y).
  2. Logarithms convert division to subtraction log(x/y)=log(x)−log(y)\log(x/y) = \log(x) - \log(y).
  3. Logarithms let you pull down powers log(xp)=plog(x)\log(x^p) = p \log(x).

The most important base for exponential and logarithmic functions in calculus is the number e≈2.71828e \approx 2.71828. This is the natural base for the logarithm and exponential function for reasons that we’ll see later when we talk about derivatives. We write ln(x)\ln(x) to denote the base-e logarithm.
